As we invite robots into places we live and work, my research aims to realize natural robot learning — experiential improvement from signals accessible to or communicable by animals and humans. I believe these methods will enable more sustainably scalable autonomy for all embodiments, not only those which we can tele-operate, model, or simulate.

This position is largely motivated by my experience in developing off-road self-driving (now Overland AI), where I felt three long-standing pillars of engineering bottlenecked scalable, unstructured autonomy and deserves new attention:

  1. Natural Task Specification. Rewards and objectives can be highly domain-specific or impossible, especially for end-users, to design then communicate.
    Robots should be capable of inferring goals from observations of the world and their users.

  2. Continual Physical Understanding. The diversity of the natural world tends to render naive generalization of static, hand-designed or pre-trained features unreliable and dangerous.
    Robots should be capable of reasoning, then learning, when it inevitably finds itself in an unfamiliar situation.

  3. End-to-End Abstractions. Many problems on deployment arose and recurred at the interfaces between sub-system modules (e.g. perception and control) where hand-designed abstractions were enforced or constrained.
    Robots should learn (not act!) end-to-end as engineering distinctions and modules blur in challenging settings.
